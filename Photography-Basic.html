PHOTOGRAPHY BASIC

How Do Camera Works ?

A camera works by letting light in through its lens. This light is then focused on a picture sensor. The image sensor catches out the light and turns it into an electrical signal. The image processor then does something with the signal. The data that was successfully dealt with has been stored as a digital image on a memory card. Adjustments are made to camera settings like aperture, shutter speed, ISO, and focus to control the lighting and get the picture you want. Also, cameras may have viewfinders or LCD screens that let you frame the shot and see what it will look like.

APERTURE

Aperture

The aperture sets up how big the lens's hole is. It changes how much light gets into the camera and the depth of field, which is the range of how sharp the picture is.

Aperture and f-stops

When the aperture is wide (small f-number), more light comes in and the depth of field is short. When the aperture is narrow (large f-number), less light comes in and the depth of field is deeper. A standard scale : ​ f/1.4, f/2, f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8, f/11, f/16, f/22

Shutter Speed

The shutter's speed controls how long the shutter remain is open, allowing light to enter the camera's sensor. The image's exposure and blur due to motion are adjusted. Freezing motion requires a fast shutter speed (e.g., 1/1000s), whereas blurring it requires a slow one (e.g., 1/30s).

Iso Value

ISO stands for International Organization for Standardization. The ISO value reflects how light-sensitive the camera's sensor is. The sensor becomes more light-sensitive at higher ISO settings, letting you to take pictures even in dim light. However, increasing the ISO level might cause digital noise, which affects the picture quality.

EXPOSURE TRIANGLE

Aperture, shutter speed, and ISO are the three most essential controls of a camera, and in photography, they represent that is known as the exposure triangle. Accurate exposure can only be achieved by mastering the interplay between these controls.

The exposure triangle shows the relationship between these three settings. Changing any one of the settings will need you to readjust the others to keep the exposure the same. Take, as an instance:

More light enters the camera when the aperture increases and opens up. You may need to either slow down the shutter speed (faster) or turn down the ISO to prevent overexposure.

Reducing the shutter speed (longer exposure) allows more light to reach the sensor. Overexposure may be avoided by either reducing the aperture (making it smaller) or the ISO.

By increasing the ISO (making it more sensitive), less light is needed to properly expose the sensor. To prevent your photos from being overexposed, try increasing the shutter speed (faster) or decreasing the aperture (narrower).

By adjusting these three settings in together, you may change depth of field, motion blur, and noise in your photos without affecting exposure. If you want to learn how these controls interact to produce certain effects in your photographs, experimentation and practise are important.

SUMMARY EXPOSURE

The term "exposure" is used in photography to describe the quantity of light that reaches the picture sensor. Aperture, shutter speed, and ISO are the most important controls. The quantity of light entering the lens is controlled by its aperture. The quantity of light that reaches the sensor is controlled by the shutter speed, which is a function of how long the shutter is open. Light sensitivity is controlled by the ISO setting. By adjusting these settings, you can ensure that your photographs are properly exposed, neither over- nor underexposed.

RECOMMENDATION

Different photographers will have different preferences in terms of equipment, shooting methods, and budget limitations. Here, however, are a few suggestions for beginner photographers.

BODY ​CAMERA

DSLR (Digital Single Lens Reflex)

DSLR cameras use a mirror and prism mechanism that lets you look through an optical viewfinder to see the scene. They have manual controls, interchangeable lenses, and superb picture quality. Professional photographers and fans who value flexibility and durability use DSLRs.

Mirrorless

Compact system cameras (sometimes known as mirrorless cameras) lack a mirror and an optical viewfinder. Electronic viewfinders (EVFs) or LCD displays are used instead for composing and previewing images. Mirrorless cameras have advanced features and interchangeable lenses, but are more compact, lighter, and easier to carry than DSLRs. Photographers who place a high value on getting around and cutting-edge features frequently move toward them.

compact / point-and-shoot

Compact cameras, sometimes known as point-and-shoots, are intended to be easy to use. They typically feature a fixed lens and are compact and lightweight. These cameras are great for daily usage and casual photography because to their automated settings and minimal manual controls.

medium format

The bigger sensors used in medium format cameras allow for greater resolution and better overall picture quality than those found in DSLRs and mirrorless cameras. They are frequently used in commercial, studio, and landscape photography, all of which place a high value on sharpness and detail. Professional photographers often use bigger, more costly medium format cameras.

film camera

Film cameras don't use digital sensors to take pictures. Instead, they use traditional photographic film. There are different sizes, such as 35mm, medium format, and large format. Film cameras are liked by photographers, artists, and people who want a more traditional way to take photos because of their unique look.

Each type of camera body has its own strengths and can be used for various types of photography and needs. When picking the right camera body for your needs, it's important to think about elements like picture quality, control choices, flexibility, and price.

CAMERA LENS

Prime Lens

Prime lenses have a set focal length, which means they can't be zoomed. They have great picture quality, larger maximum apertures, and are often chosen because of how sharp they are and how well they work in low light. Prime lenses, which come in focal lengths like 35mm, 50mm, and 85mm, are often used for portraits, street photos, and getting a certain look or style.

Zoom Lens

Zoom lenses have a focal length that can be changed, so you can change the magnification without changing lenses. You can zoom in to get close-up shots or zoom out to get wide-angle shots. Standard zoom lenses, like 24-70mm, are popular for general photography, while long zoom lenses, like 70-200mm, are great for sports, wildlife, and other cases where you need to catch faraway objects.

Wide-Angle Lens

The focal length of a wide-angle lens is usually less than 35mm on a full-frame camera. They have a bigger field of view, which lets you take pictures of large scenery, buildings, and rooms. Wide-angle lenses also make things look bigger than they are, which makes them great for artistic compositions and bringing attention to things in the center.

Telephoto Lens

Telephoto lenses have a focal length that is longer, like 70-200mm or 100-400mm. They let you zoom in and take pictures of things far away, which makes them popular for taking pictures of sports, wildlife, and events. Telephoto lenses also make things look closer together even though they are far away.

Macro Lens

Macro lenses are made for close-up photos, so they let you get close to small things like flowers, insects, or textures and catch fine details. They have a high magnification level and good picture sharpness at close focusing distances. This lets you see small details that are hard to see with the naked eye.

Fish-Eye Lens

Fish-eye lenses have a very wide field of view, usually between 8 and 16 mm. They give you a very skewed and twisted view, letting you see a large area and giving you a unique look. Fish-eye glasses are often used in art and creativity to make pictures that look distorted or strange.

Tilt-Shift Lens

Tilt-shift lenses have special settings for changing the angle of view and putting the focus where you want it. They let you change the plane of focus and the depth of field. This makes them popular in building photos, where they can be used to fix lines that meet or make things look like they are smaller.

Tripod

For steady shots, long exposures, and self-portraits, you need a strong tripod. Look for one that is stable, the right height, and easy to change.

Memory Card

Spend money on high-quality, reliable memory cards that can hold enough information and can be read and written quickly. SD or CF cards are popular choices, but make sure that your camera is compatible with them

Bag for Camera

 A sturdy bag will keep your gear safe and make it easy to move. Look for one with soft straps, cushion sections, and dividers that you can change.

Extra Batteries

If you have extra batteries for your camera, you won't run out of power when you're taking for a long time. Get original batteries or cells from a reliable third party that work with your camera type.

Cleaning Kit

A cleaning kit with a fan brush, cotton cloth, lens cleaning solution, and tools for cleaning the sensor will help you keep your camera and lenses clean.

Cleaning Kit

Consider buying important filters like a UV filter to protect your lens and a circle polarizer filter to stop shadows and make colours pop when you're taking pictures outside.
